# Scenario 3 Output — Leader seat, EN, transcript review

**Input:** Leader provides a 1:1 transcript with their head of BD. Outlicensing conversions stalled — 1 of 5 term sheets converted in Q3 vs. 2 of 4 last year. The BD head's answers in the transcript are notably vague, deflecting to "market conditions" and "I'd have to check."

## Artifact 1 — Diagnosis MD

# Strategic Alignment Diagnosis

**Seat detected:** Leader
**Inputs triaged:** A 1:1 transcript with the head of BD; an outcome metric (1/5 vs prior 2/4); your prior expectations of the BD head.
**Language:** EN
**Confidence in overall diagnosis:** High on the *transcript-level* patterns (the text speaks plainly). Medium on root cause — the transcript is one data point, and several patterns are consistent with it.

---

## Headline

The transcript shows a BD head who either does not know the status of the deals they own, or knows but won't surface it — and is attributing the underperformance to external market conditions rather than to anything they're doing or not doing. Both readings have the same first-step corrective move, but the diagnoses diverge after that.

---

## Patterns detected

### Selective reporting — Severity 4

**Evidence.** When asked "what's holding them up specifically," answer: "Different things for each one. Hard to generalize." When asked about Boston Pharma: "I'd have to check my notes on where that one is exactly. I think there was a question about the IP carve-out." When asked about the Shanghai group: "That one's complicated. Their team had some turnover." Three separate deal-specific questions, three deflections of escalating vagueness.

The opening framing — "market conditions, you know" — sets up an external attribution before any deal-specific facts are offered. That's the structural tell.

**Root cause hypothesis.** Either (a) review/comp culture punishes named bad news, so the learned behavior is to keep deal status fuzzy until the deal closes or dies, or (b) the BD head doesn't actually track deal status with enough granularity to answer the question — the deflection is sincere, not strategic. Both are systemic, not character.

**What would change my mind.** If the next 1:1, having had time to "check notes," the BD head returns with a granular per-deal status with named blockers and last-touch dates, the reporting pattern is fixable through process rather than a deeper issue. If the granularity still doesn't appear, it's the deeper issue.

### Effort displacement (probable) — Severity 3

**Evidence.** The BD head doesn't know the status of three specific deals they own. They closed the conversation with "it's mostly out of our hands. Markets will turn." If you don't know the status, you can't move the deal forward — by definition, whatever they're spending their time on isn't deal advancement.

**Root cause hypothesis.** When the work feels stalled and external, the natural response is to redirect effort to things that feel more tractable (internal meetings, pipeline development for next year, hiring, conference prep). Activity stays high; outcome velocity on the current deals drops.

**What would change my mind.** If you can ask the BD head "what specific actions did you take on Boston Pharma in the last two weeks" and get a substantive answer, this diagnosis weakens. If you can't, it strengthens.

### Information asymmetry — Severity 2

**Evidence.** "It's mostly out of our hands. Markets will turn." This is a one-sided narrative that you, as a non-BD person, would have a hard time second-sourcing without an external benchmark. Other companies' outlicensing conversion rates in the same period, or peer BD heads' frame on market conditions, would tell you whether "markets" is doing real explanatory work or covering for execution.

**Root cause hypothesis.** Not necessarily intentional — but the asymmetry is being used (consciously or not) as a shield against drilling into specifics.

**What would change my mind.** If you bring in a peer benchmark or external BD advisor and they confirm market conditions explain most of the gap, the asymmetry isn't being exploited — it's reality. If they don't, the explanation is convenient rather than true.

---

## Is the leader the source?

**Partly.** Two questions to honest-answer:

1. **Did you ever establish the expectation that the BD head should know deal status at any moment?** If the answer is no — if your prior reviews focused on big-picture pipeline and quarterly closings rather than weekly deal mechanics — then the BD head has been operating in a culture where deal status detail isn't expected to be top-of-mind. The transcript is then a sign of mis-set expectations, not of avoidance.

2. **Did you have a written deal status review with named blockers per deal as a standing artifact?** If no, you're discovering deal status through a verbal channel that's structurally low-fidelity. The fix is partly upstream: build the artifact, don't rely on memory.

If both answers are no, the corrective conversation needs to lead with you owning the upstream gap before asking the BD head to change behavior. Not as performance theater — because if you don't, the BD head will (correctly) hear the conversation as "you didn't tell me you wanted this and now you're upset" and the behavior change won't stick.

---

## What is NOT the problem

- **It's not (yet) bad faith.** The transcript is consistent with both adversarial and benign explanations. Don't fix on bad-faith before testing the structural ones.
- **It's not the BD head's general competence.** Last year they ran 2/4 — they can close. Something has changed in either the deals, the process, or the engagement.
- **Markets may genuinely be soft.** Don't pretend external conditions don't exist. The question is whether they explain the gap or whether they're being used as the explanation.

## Artifact 2 — Corrective Message Draft

**Recipient:** Head of BD
**Suggested channel:** 1:1 conversation, in person if possible. The previous 1:1 already happened — this is the second touch, where you bring structure rather than re-litigate.
**Suggested timing:** This week's 1:1, with the written request below sent 24-48 hours ahead so they have time to actually look up the information rather than deflect again.

---

### Primary version

**Pre-message (24-48h before the 1:1):**

> "For tomorrow's 1:1, I want to walk through our open outlicensing deals one by one. For each of the five term sheets — Genscript, the Shanghai group, Boston Pharma, [4], [5] — please bring: (a) current status in one sentence, (b) the specific blocker keeping it from closing, (c) the named individual on their side responsible for unblocking, (d) date of your last substantive contact with that person, and (e) the next action and who owns it. I'm not asking for forecasts — just the current ground truth."

**In the 1:1:**

> "Thanks for pulling this together. Before we go deal by deal, I want to name something from our last conversation that I should have said directly. When I asked about specific deals last week, the answers I got were mostly 'I'd have to check' or 'it's complicated.' I don't think you were trying to dodge me — but I also realized I haven't been clear that I need you to be on top of the deal-by-deal mechanics, not just the pipeline aggregate. That's been an unstated expectation, and that's on me.
>
> Here's what I want to change going forward. I want a weekly written deal status with the five fields from yesterday's note — that's a Friday deliverable. Then this 1:1 stops being a status meeting and becomes a problem-solving meeting where we focus on the deals that are stuck and what we can do about them.
>
> On the 1-of-5 number this quarter: I want us to look honestly at how much of that is market and how much is execution. I'll bring in [name a peer / external advisor] to give us a benchmark on what peers are converting at. Not to second-guess you — to give us both a shared picture of what's the market and what's us."

---

### Alternative versions

**Firmer** (if you've established the deal-status expectation before and the transcript signals regression, not a first-time miscalibration):

> "We have to fix something. When I asked about specific deals last week, three out of three answers were variants of 'I'd have to check' or 'it's complicated.' That's not a status, that's a deflection — and the 1-of-5 conversion is consistent with the team not driving deals as hard as we did last year. Starting this week: weekly written deal status, Friday, five fields I sent. Next 1:1 we work the stuck deals. If a deal is stuck because of markets, you tell me which lever isn't available and we figure out alternatives. If we can't move from 1-of-5 trajectory in the next 30 days, we revisit how this team is structured."

**Softer / exploratory** (if you suspect substantial leader-source contribution):

> "I want to figure something out with you. Last week's conversation didn't go great — I asked about specific deals and you gave general answers, and I went away frustrated. I think part of that is on me — I don't think I've made clear that I want to be in the weeds on each deal weekly, not just on the aggregate pipeline. Can we design a process together that gives me visibility into per-deal mechanics without making your job feel surveilled? I have an idea for a weekly written status — but I'd rather build it with you than impose it."

---

### Lines NOT to use

- **"I need you to be more accountable."** Moralizes. "Accountable" without specifics is a slur dressed as feedback. Name the artifact you want (weekly written status) — that's accountability operationalized.
- **"It feels like you're not engaged."** Speculates about internal state. Stay with the observable: the answers in the transcript, the conversion ratio.
- **"Trust" of any form.** The trust frame escalates instead of correcting. The behavior you want is procedural — go procedural.
- **"This isn't like you."** Triggers defensiveness, even if true.

## Tradeoffs explicitly accepted

- **The BD head spends ~3-4 hours/week on the written status artifact.** This is real overhead. We accept it because the visibility was missing and verbal channels weren't producing it.
- **Conference / pipeline-development activity gets deprioritized this quarter** in favor of focus on the 5 stuck deals + 2 new outreach. We name what is *not* being done — Q4 pipeline-building work, speaking engagements, junior BD coaching — and accept that explicitly.

## Counterfactual — what the org gets if we succeed

If KR1 lands, you have ongoing visibility into deal mechanics that makes the next quarter's diagnosis (whatever it is) easier and faster. If KR3 lands, you've broken the 1-of-5 narrative — even one close shifts the trajectory and team morale. If KR2 lands and shows market explains most of the gap, you've correctly calibrated expectations and avoided over-correcting on the BD head. The shared scoreboard prevents the same conversation from recurring next quarter.

## What would tell us the plan is wrong

- **If after 2 weeks the written status is consistently late, missing fields, or vague,** the issue is not process — it's that the BD head either can't or won't operate at this level of detail. That's a role-fit decision, not a coaching one.
- **If KR2 shows peers are converting at 40-50% and you're at 20%,** market doesn't explain the gap. The corrective conversation has to escalate.
- **If KR3 lands and the close was almost entirely the deal counterparty re-engaging on their own initiative (i.e., the BD head didn't materially move it),** the conversion is real but the underlying execution issue isn't fixed — re-diagnose.
