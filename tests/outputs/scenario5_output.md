## 工件 1 — Disagreement Diagnosis (not a "misalignment" diagnosis)

# Strategic Disagreement Analysis

**Seat:** Subordinate, raising a strategic concern openly.
**Inputs triaged:** Your stated objection (timing of the AI-native transition); the fact that you've raised it twice already; the existence of paying customers on the current pipeline; the not-prod-ready state of the AI version.
**Language:** CN-dominant mixed register
**Confidence in overall framing:** High — you've done the diagnostic work yourself ("I genuinely don't think this is the right move right now"). The engine's job is structuring how to make that case visible, not second-guessing whether you're right.

---

## Core observation

You and your CEO are looking at the same situation through different framings:

- **CEO's frame (most likely):** AI-native is the future of target discovery. The org that doesn't transition becomes commoditized. Every quarter of delay is a quarter of compounding disadvantage. The customers on the existing pipeline are valuable but not strategically determinative; the next decade is.

- **Your frame:** Existing customers are real revenue and real validation right now. AI-native isn't ready for prod. Switching now means a revenue gap, a credibility gap with current customers, and a forced-march on tech that isn't done. The right move is parallel rather than replace.

**Both frames are defensible.** This isn't a competence disagreement — it's a risk-posture and time-horizon disagreement. CEO's frame weights long-horizon strategic risk; yours weights medium-horizon execution risk. The right answer depends on facts neither of you has fully on the table yet.

The fact that you've raised concerns twice and the CEO is still pushing means the concerns weren't *legible enough* — they probably came across as "I'm worried" rather than as "here's the specific quantified risk and here's the falsifiable path that resolves it." That's fixable.

---

## What "raising it twice didn't work" likely means

Three possibilities, ordered by likelihood:

1. **Your concerns sounded like risk-aversion rather than strategy.** CEOs hear "I'm not ready" frequently and discount it — it's the modal response to any ambitious push. To break through, the concern has to come with numbers (current revenue at risk, specific readiness gaps with criteria), not just qualitative judgment.

2. **The CEO has information you don't have.** They may know about investor pressure, a competitive announcement, a board commitment, or a customer comment that makes the timing urgent in a way they haven't shared. If so, your case has to invite them to share that — not just argue against the timing.

3. **The CEO has already decided and the "discussion" is performative.** This is the worst case but worth naming. If true, your job changes from "make the case" to "make sure the decision is documented and bounded, and decide whether you can execute it in good faith."

You can probably identify which is closest by how the previous two conversations felt. If they ended with the CEO acknowledging your points and then restating the push, it's likely 1 or 2. If they ended with the CEO interrupting or seeming impatient, it's likely 3.

---

## What you should and should not do

**Do:**
- Surface the disagreement in writing. Verbal dissent vanishes; written dissent creates a forcing function.
- Quantify both sides — current revenue, current customer count, AI readiness gap with specific criteria.
- Propose a falsifiable middle path that lets the CEO commit to the AI direction while preserving the existing pipeline's value for a defined period.
- Make clear that you will execute whichever path the CEO decides, but that you're raising the concern so the decision is informed.

**Don't:**
- 阳奉阴违 (outwardly comply, inwardly defy). 你说了你不想这么做，that instinct is correct. Slow-rolling the transition while saying you're on board is the worst available option — it gives the CEO the wrong information about progress, and when the gap surfaces (it will), the failure looks like your failure, not the strategy's.
- Make this an interpersonal conflict. Stay strategic, stay falsifiable.
- Position yourself as the protector of the past. The case is not "the old way is good." The case is "here's the risk profile of the transition and here's a path that preserves the upside while managing the downside."
- Issue ultimatums (resignation hints, etc.). Premature.

### Alternative versions

**Firmer** (if you have strong evidence the CEO is dismissing without engaging):

> Drop the "to be clear, I don't oppose AI-native" preamble. Lead with: "I've raised this twice verbally without changing the trajectory. I want to make the case in writing so it can be engaged with deliberately." Then go straight to the quantified concerns. End with a more pointed ask: "If you've already decided and this discussion is closed, I'd like to know — that's a different conversation from 'we're still weighing this.'"

**Softer / exploratory** (if you suspect possibility #2 from the diagnosis — there's information you don't have):

> Frame the memo as questions rather than positions. "I want to make sure I understand the strategic urgency — what's driving the timing on AI-native?" Then lay out the concerns as "things I'd want to understand how we handle." Less assertive, more invitational. Use this if you trust the CEO will engage in good faith and the lack of traction has been about misframing rather than disregard.

### 不要 use的措辞

- **"我担心 customers won't like it."** 没有数字, no specifics — sounds like protecting the past. Replace with quantified customer impact.
- **"AI还不行."** 太general. Replace with the specific readiness gap criteria.
- **任何 emotional escalation** — "I don't feel heard," "我们 keep ignoring这个" — these route to the relationship rather than the strategy. Stay strategic.
- **"团队 won't be able to do this."** Don't speak for the team. Speak for the strategy.
- **Implicit resignation threats** — "I'm not sure I can lead this if..." Premature, and weakens the strategic argument by mixing it with leverage.

## Final operating note

If the CEO's response to the memo is to reaffirm A without engaging the specifics — same conversation as before, just at higher volume — that's a third data point. At that point the question shifts from "how do I make my case" to "can I execute A in good faith." 这是a different question, and the engine isn't the right tool to answer it. Honest options are: (a) execute A fully and own the risks, including the ones that materialize; (b) raise the question of whether you're the right leader for this transition, openly; (c) ask for what would have to be true for you to commit fully (which may surface that the gap is unbridgeable). 不要 default to阳奉阴违 — that's the option that's worst for the org and for you.
